# AthletIQ Volleyball Hitting Approach Research Framework

Last updated: 2026-06-24

Purpose: define a coach-reviewable, evidence-informed framework for AthletIQ's volleyball hitting approach analysis engine. This document is not a scoring formula. It separates what is supported by research/coaching consensus from what still needs validation.

## Executive Summary

The most defensible AthletIQ V1 approach metrics are not "perfect form" metrics. They are video-observable quality gates and timing landmarks:

- full-body visibility
- approach start
- step rhythm trend
- penultimate-to-plant sequence
- takeoff frame
- peak jump frame
- landing frame
- approximate hip/center-of-body lowering before takeoff
- arm-swing timing relative to plant/takeoff
- landing visibility and basic balance

AthletIQ should not initially claim to measure force production, true center of mass, joint torques, shoulder kinetics, ball velocity, injury risk, or elite-equivalent mechanics from ordinary side-view phone video.

## Research Area 1: Hitting Approach Mechanics

### Approach Rhythm

Why it matters:

- The approach connects timing to the set and prepares horizontal speed for conversion into vertical jump.
- Coaching consensus favors a progressive rhythm rather than a flat-speed approach. Common language is "slow-to-fast," "load then explode," or "controlled acceleration."
- Research on approach spike jumps supports the importance of the approach, but one study of NCAA female players found that approach jump height exceeded standing countermovement jump height while horizontal center-of-mass velocity alone did not explain the difference. This means AthletIQ should not reward speed by itself.

Elite pattern:

- Controlled first step or initiation.
- Increasing tempo into the last two contacts.
- Set-dependent timing: first-tempo, second-tempo, and third-tempo attacks differ.

Common mistakes:

- Starting too early or too late relative to the set.
- Rushing with no usable load.
- Decelerating before plant.
- Choppy last two steps.

What coaches look for:

- Smooth acceleration.
- Last two steps that create both positioning and loading.
- On-time jump under/behind the ball, not drifting uncontrollably.

Video measurements:

- Hip-center horizontal displacement over time.
- Step contact timestamps.
- Ratio of early-step duration to last-two-step duration.
- Presence of deceleration or stutter before plant.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Side-view: moderate for rhythm if feet are visible.
- Front/broadcast: low.

### Approach Speed

Why it matters:

- Horizontal approach can improve spike jump height, but speed must be controlled and converted into vertical impulse.
- Research comparing approach jumps and standing jumps supports that approach technique can increase jump height. Research also cautions that horizontal approach velocity alone may not be the determining factor.

Elite pattern:

- Fast enough to create useful momentum.
- Controlled enough to allow a stable two-foot takeoff.

Common mistakes:

- Too slow: poor momentum and weak load.
- Too fast: forward drift, low contact, or net violation risk.

Video measurements:

- Hip-center horizontal velocity trend.
- Horizontal drift from takeoff to landing.
- Distance traveled in last 0.5-1.0 seconds before takeoff.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate only for relative trend inside one video.
- Low for absolute speed unless court scale is calibrated.

### Step Sequence

Why it matters:

- Volleyball attacking commonly uses 3-, 4-, or 5-step approaches depending on position, tempo, and transition context.
- The final two steps are the most important for converting approach momentum into jump.

Elite pattern:

- Consistent step order for the athlete's handedness and tactical context.
- A clear penultimate step and plant step before takeoff.

Common mistakes:

- Wrong-footed final sequence.
- Extra adjustment steps.
- No clear final plant.

Video measurements:

- Foot contact events.
- Foot order.
- Step duration and step length estimates.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Reliable only when both feet are visible.
- Low when feet are occluded, cropped, or blurred.

### Penultimate Step

Why it matters:

- The penultimate step is the second-to-last ground contact before plant/takeoff.
- Coaching consensus treats it as the load step: it helps lower the body, organize braking, and prepare the final plant.
- Volleyball-specific research often discusses spike jump kinematics and last-step mechanics, but the exact term "penultimate step" is more common in coaching language than in paper titles. AthletIQ should therefore label this as research-adjacent plus coach-consensus until internally validated.

Elite pattern:

- Often longer than the plant step.
- Helps lower the hips/center of body.
- Arms are commonly driven back during or near the last two steps.

Common mistakes:

- Penultimate step too short.
- Athlete stays tall through the final two contacts.
- Arms late or disconnected from foot contacts.

Video measurements:

- Relative penultimate step length versus plant step length.
- Hip-center vertical lowering from penultimate contact to plant/takeoff.
- Arm-back timing relative to penultimate/plant.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate from clean side-view video.
- Do not score absolute ideal length without coach-validated thresholds.

### Plant Step

Why it matters:

- Final ground contact organizes braking and two-foot takeoff.
- A stable plant helps redirect horizontal momentum vertically.

Elite pattern:

- Fast final contact after the penultimate.
- Both feet participate in takeoff for standard front-row attacks.
- Foot contacts are close enough in time to behave as a coordinated block/jump.

Common mistakes:

- Plant too far forward, causing forward drift.
- Plant too narrow or unstable.
- Last contact too slow.

Video measurements:

- Timestamp of final foot contact before airborne phase.
- Foot spacing at plant.
- Hip horizontal velocity reduction before takeoff.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate with foot visibility.
- Low if shoes are hidden by court lines, other players, or motion blur.

### Takeoff Mechanics

Why it matters:

- Takeoff is where approach momentum, lower-body loading, arm swing, and torso posture combine.
- Volleyball spike-jump research supports the importance of lower-extremity kinematics, hip/knee/ankle contribution, and coordinated takeoff mechanics.

Elite pattern:

- Visible lowering before extension.
- Trunk stays controlled rather than collapsing forward.
- Arms contribute to upward momentum and hitting preparation.

Common mistakes:

- Insufficient load.
- Excessive forward lean.
- Jumping forward instead of upward.
- Late arms.

Video measurements:

- Hip-center lowest point before takeoff.
- Knee/hip angle proxy from 2D pose.
- Torso angle at plant/takeoff.
- Takeoff timestamp: last frame before both feet leave ground.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate for timing and gross posture.
- Low for true joint angles/forces from single-camera video.

### Arm Swing Mechanics

Why it matters:

- Upper-body studies show volleyball spike mechanics involve coordinated overarm motion and proximal-to-distal sequencing.
- Shoulder and elbow loads are meaningful in volleyball spikes; arm swing assessment should therefore be cautious and not framed as medical injury screening.

Elite pattern:

- Arms move back during the load and then up/forward with takeoff.
- Hitting arm prepares into a loaded/cocked position after takeoff.
- Contact occurs with a high reach when ball data is visible.

Common mistakes:

- Arms start too late.
- No backswing/load.
- Hitting elbow drops excessively.
- Early swing before takeoff.

Video measurements:

- Wrist/elbow backward movement relative to penultimate and plant.
- Arm-back maximum timestamp.
- Shoulder-wrist vertical/horizontal relationships near takeoff.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate for arm-swing timing from side view if arms are visible.
- Low for true shoulder kinetics, internal rotation velocity, or injury risk.

### Torso Positioning

Why it matters:

- Torso posture affects approach-to-jump transfer and hitting preparation.
- Landing and ACL literature also shows trunk position can influence lower-extremity mechanics, though AthletIQ should not infer injury risk from a single side-view clip.

Elite pattern:

- Controlled forward lean during approach.
- No collapse at plant.
- Torso organizes upward drive and hitting preparation.

Common mistakes:

- Excessive forward lean at takeoff.
- Arching/flaring without control.
- Rotating out of the approach lane too early.

Video measurements:

- Shoulder-hip line angle from side view.
- Torso angle change from penultimate to takeoff.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Moderate for gross sagittal-plane torso angle.
- Low for rotation from side view.

### Landing Mechanics

Why it matters:

- Volleyball has high landing volume. Landing technique research and feedback studies support using video/verbal feedback to modify landing biomechanics.
- Landing control matters for safe training feedback, but AthletIQ should avoid diagnosing injury risk.

Elite pattern:

- Land balanced.
- Absorb through hips/knees/ankles.
- Avoid uncontrolled forward drift or one-leg collapse when two-foot landing is expected.

Common mistakes:

- Stiff landing.
- One-foot overload.
- Knees collapse inward, but this is hard to confirm from side view.
- Landing outside the video frame.

Video measurements:

- Landing timestamp.
- Whether landing is visible.
- Approximate knee/hip flexion after ground contact.
- Horizontal drift from takeoff to landing.
- Time to stabilize, if enough frames are available.

AthletIQ confidence:

- Reliable for "landing visible/not visible" and approximate landing time.
- Moderate for sagittal-plane landing softness.
- Low for valgus/asymmetry from side-view only.

## Research Area 2: Penultimate Step

Purpose:

- Create body lowering and braking/setup before final plant.
- Establish rhythm for the final two steps.
- Help coordinate arm backswing and takeoff preparation.

Biomechanical role:

- Helps transition from horizontal approach to vertical jump.
- May allow eccentric loading of lower extremity joints before explosive extension.
- Interacts with step length, hip lowering, torso control, and arm timing.

Common coaching cues:

- "Long-short."
- "Big penultimate, quick plant."
- "Load before you explode."
- "Arms back as you load."

Common mistakes:

- Short penultimate step.
- Tall final two steps.
- Arms late.
- Stutter step before plant.

Evidence level:

- Moderate coaching consensus.
- Moderate indirect biomechanics support from spike-jump kinematics and approach-jump literature.
- Needs coach-reviewed AthletIQ validation before numeric scoring.

AthletIQ video evaluation:

- Detect penultimate contact as second-to-last visible foot contact before takeoff.
- Compute relative step duration and step displacement.
- Check whether hip-center lowers between penultimate contact and takeoff.
- Warn instead of score if feet are not visible.

## Research Area 3: Arm Swing Timing

What research supports:

- Volleyball spike and serve place meaningful kinetic demand on the upper limb.
- Overarm-motion research supports proximal-to-distal sequencing in volleyball spikes.
- Upper-limb kinematic studies show spike type and player development affect shoulder, elbow, wrist, and trunk variables.

Common coaching cues:

- "Arms back on the load."
- "Swing both arms through takeoff."
- "Reach high."
- "Load the bow."

Common mistakes:

- Arms still forward at plant.
- Arm swing starts after takeoff.
- Hitting arm drops or disconnects from torso.

Observable indicators:

- Timing of wrists moving behind hips/trunk.
- Arm-back maximum before takeoff.
- Arm-up motion starts near plant/takeoff.

AthletIQ method:

- Track wrist, elbow, shoulder landmarks.
- Estimate arm-backswing event as local maximum backward wrist displacement relative to shoulder/hip.
- Compare arm-backswing event to penultimate, plant, and takeoff events.
- Score only timing bands after coach validation; initially report "early/on-time/late/unclear."

## Research Area 4: Takeoff Mechanics

### Hip Loading

Importance:

- Hip lowering and extension contribute to jump mechanics and approach-to-vertical transfer.

Video indicator:

- Hip-center y-coordinate drops before takeoff and rises rapidly after extension.

Confidence:

- Moderate for gross lowering.
- Low for true hip torque/power.

### Knee Loading

Importance:

- Knee flexion/extension participates in countermovement and force production.

Video indicator:

- Knee angle proxy at plant and lowest hip position.

Confidence:

- Moderate in side view with visible knees.
- Low if shorts, occlusion, or camera angle obscure landmarks.

### Center-of-Mass Lowering

Importance:

- Coaches often describe "load" before "explode"; biomechanics would ideally measure COM.

Video indicator:

- Use hip center as a proxy, not true COM.

Confidence:

- Moderate as "hip-center lowering."
- Do not call it true COM without full-body model validation.

### Torso Posture

Importance:

- Excessive forward lean can reduce vertical orientation and complicate hitting preparation.

Video indicator:

- Shoulder-hip line angle at plant and takeoff.

Confidence:

- Moderate from side view.

### Vertical Force Production

Importance:

- True determinant of jump outcome.

Video indicator:

- Not directly observable from ordinary video.
- Approximate outcomes: jump height proxy, flight time, peak hip displacement.

Confidence:

- Low without force plate, validated scale, frame rate, and calibration.

## Research Area 5: Landing Mechanics

What can be evaluated from side view:

- Whether landing is visible.
- Landing frame.
- One-foot versus two-foot landing if feet visible.
- Approximate knee/hip flexion in sagittal plane.
- Excessive forward drift after takeoff.
- Immediate loss of balance if visible.

What cannot be evaluated reliably:

- Frontal-plane knee valgus.
- Left/right force asymmetry.
- ACL injury risk.
- Ground reaction force.
- Joint moments.

Product rule:

- AthletIQ can give landing-control observations and visibility warnings.
- AthletIQ must not diagnose injury risk or medical readiness.

## Research Area 6: Elite Athlete Study

Shared principles across elite hitters:

- They create a consistent approach rhythm that matches set tempo.
- They are on time to the ball, not simply fast.
- They use the last two steps to load and organize takeoff.
- They coordinate arm swing with takeoff.
- They contact high relative to their body and tactical context.
- They land and recover into the next play.

Areas of variation:

- Three-, four-, or five-step approach.
- Tempo by position and play call.
- Arm swing style.
- Amount of forward drift.
- One-foot slide attacks versus two-foot standard attacks.
- Left-handed versus right-handed approach patterns.
- Body size and role-specific mechanics.

Product implication:

- AthletIQ should compare athletes to principles, not claim they should copy a specific pro.
- Pro-player references can be used for study, but movement matching requires validated landmark datasets.

## Research Area 7: Coaching Consensus

Recurring themes from coaching literature and clinics:

- Timing to the set is central.
- Approach rhythm is usually taught as progressive acceleration.
- Final two steps matter most.
- Penultimate step loads; plant step redirects.
- Arms should be integrated with the lower body.
- Athletes should reach high and contact in front/above appropriate to the set.
- Landing should be balanced and controlled.
- Coaches prefer simple cues that athletes can act on immediately.

Common coach-review checklist:

- Did the athlete start at the right time?
- Were the feet visible and organized?
- Was there a clear load before takeoff?
- Did the arms help the jump?
- Did the athlete jump vertically enough for the tactical goal?
- Was the landing safe enough to keep training?

## Research Area 9: Measurement Feasibility

### Reliable From Clean Side-View Video

- Video quality gates: feet visible, full body visible, athlete off-screen percentage.
- Approximate approach start.
- Takeoff timestamp.
- Peak jump timestamp.
- Landing timestamp if visible.
- Missing landmark percentage.
- Basic foot visibility percentage.

### Moderate Confidence

- Step timing and final two foot contacts.
- Penultimate step relative length.
- Plant timing.
- Hip-center lowering before takeoff.
- Torso angle in sagittal plane.
- Arm-backswing timing relative to plant/takeoff.
- Forward drift from takeoff to landing.
- Landing softness in sagittal plane.

### Low Confidence

- True center of mass.
- True joint angles without calibration.
- Left/right asymmetry from side view.
- Knee valgus from side view.
- Ball contact quality unless ball is tracked.
- Set timing unless setter/ball are visible.
- Approach speed in m/s without scale calibration.
- Jump height in cm without scale calibration.

### Not Realistically Measurable In V1

- Ground reaction force.
- Joint torque or power.
- Shoulder internal rotation velocity.
- ACL injury risk.
- Fatigue state.
- Professional movement similarity.
- Tactical decision quality.
- Ball velocity unless radar or calibrated ball tracking exists.

## Recommended First Metrics To Score

Score only when quality gates pass:

1. Foot visibility percentage.
2. Athlete off-screen percentage.
3. Missing pose frame percentage.
4. Takeoff detected / not detected.
5. Landing detected / not detected.
6. Phase order validity.
7. Approach rhythm consistency as a low-stakes score.
8. Penultimate step relative timing/length as a coach-review score.
9. Arm swing timing as early/on-time/late/unclear before numeric scoring.
10. Landing visibility and basic control.

## Metrics Not To Score Initially

- True force production.
- True center of mass.
- Joint torque/power.
- Injury risk.
- Knee valgus from side view.
- Spike velocity.
- Ball contact quality.
- "Moves like pro" similarity.
- Tactical quality of approach choice.
- Medical readiness or return-to-play.

## Areas Requiring Coach Validation

- What numerical thresholds define short/acceptable/long penultimate step.
- Whether arm-swing timing bands should differ by athlete level, position, handedness, and approach type.
- What amount of forward drift is acceptable by position and set type.
- Whether AthletIQ should score first-tempo middle attacks differently from third-tempo pins.
- How to word feedback so it is useful without implying medical or elite-certification claims.

## Areas Requiring Future Research

- First-party dataset of side-view volleyball approaches with coach-labeled phases.
- Multi-coach reliability study for approach phase labels.
- Comparison of MediaPipe landmarks against manually labeled video.
- Camera-angle sensitivity testing.
- Validated thresholds for youth, club, college, and adult athletes.
- Ball tracking integration for set timing and contact evaluation.
- Optional force plate / wearable integration for true loading and jump impulse.

## Product Rules For AthletIQ V1

- Always show confidence and video quality warnings.
- Do not score when feet are not visible during final two steps.
- Do not claim injury prediction.
- Do not claim professional movement similarity.
- Phrase feedback as "may indicate" or "coach should review."
- Separate profile-based matching from movement-based matching.
- Make every scoring category traceable to this framework and future coach validation notes.
